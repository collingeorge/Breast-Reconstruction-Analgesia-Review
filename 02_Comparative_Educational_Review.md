## Purpose

This review examines published literature on analgesic and anesthetic approaches in post-mastectomy breast implant revision surgery, with particular attention to opioid-sparing, multimodal, and regional anesthesia frameworks as discussed in current perioperative literature. The goal is to understand how the literature characterizes the comparative considerations, potential advantages, reported limitations, and patient-specific factors associated with different perioperative analgesic strategies in this setting.

This is not an attempt to synthesize a clinical pathway. It is an effort to understand the reasoning clinicians and researchers use when evaluating perioperative options — and to analyze how evidence quality, patient heterogeneity, and institutional variability shape those discussions.

---

## Methods Note

This work is best understood as a narrative educational review rather than a systematic review or formal meta-analysis. No PRISMA framework was applied, and source identification was not exhaustive.

Literature was identified through searches of PubMed and related databases using combinations of terms including "breast reconstruction," "implant revision," "regional anesthesia," "PECS block," "opioid-sparing anesthesia," "opioid-free anesthesia," "PONV," "multimodal analgesia," and "enhanced recovery after surgery." The date range emphasized publications from 2020 through 2025. Priority was given to society guidance, systematic reviews, randomized trials, and perioperative publications most directly relevant to breast surgery and reconstruction settings. Approximately 30 sources informed the discussion.

Evidence quality across sources is heterogeneous. Where possible, guideline-level evidence is distinguished from trial-level evidence and expert opinion, though this review does not apply a formal evidence-grading framework. Readers should consult primary sources and institutional resources for any clinical purpose.

## Background and Clinical Context

Post-mastectomy breast implant revision surgery encompasses a range of procedures including capsular contracture management, implant exchange, pocket revision, and infection-related explantation and reimplantation. These procedures present perioperative management considerations that have attracted substantial literature interest, particularly regarding analgesic strategy, postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV) prevention, and recovery quality.

The published literature on this topic intersects several active areas of perioperative medicine: enhanced recovery after surgery (ERAS) pathways, opioid stewardship, regional anesthesia expansion, and multimodal analgesia. Understanding how these intersect in the context of breast reconstruction revision is the organizing question of this review.

Perioperative management in this setting involves collaboration across surgical, anesthesia, and nursing teams. Decisions about analgesic strategy, regional technique, and postoperative monitoring are highly individualized and institution-dependent. The discussion below reflects how these decisions are framed in the published literature — not how they should be made in any specific clinical context.

## Section 1: Multimodal Analgesia — Conceptual Framework

### 1.1 General Principles in Published Literature

ERAS-aligned literature commonly describes multimodal analgesia as a foundational concept in perioperative pain management (ERAS Society, 2023; PROSPECT Working Group, 2022). The core principle, as articulated in multiple society publications, is that combining analgesic agents with different mechanisms of action may reduce reliance on any single agent class — including opioids — while maintaining or improving analgesia quality.

Agents commonly discussed in the multimodal analgesia literature for breast surgery settings include acetaminophen, NSAIDs and COX-2 inhibitors, alpha-2 agonists (dexmedetomidine), NMDA receptor antagonists (ketamine), sodium channel modulators (intravenous lidocaine), regional anesthetics, and glucocorticoids (dexamethasone). The evidence base for individual agents varies considerably in depth and consistency across published studies.

### 1.2 ERAS Society Guidance

The ERAS Society has published pathway recommendations for breast reconstruction that emphasize opioid-minimizing approaches, preoperative optimization, and early mobilization (ERAS Society, 2023). These publications are frequently referenced in the perioperative literature as a framing context for multimodal analgesic discussions. Applicability to specific patients and procedures requires individualized clinical judgment by the treating team and integration with local institutional protocols.

### 1.3 Evidence Heterogeneity

A limitation noted across the literature is heterogeneity in study populations, outcome definitions, analgesic combinations, and institutional practices. Findings from one institutional or procedural context do not translate automatically to others. This review reflects that heterogeneity and does not attempt to resolve it through synthesis of a single approach.

## Section 2: Regional Anesthesia for Breast Surgery

### 2.1 Pectoral Nerve Blocks (PECS I and II)

The pectoral nerve block techniques, first described by Blanco in 2012, have been the subject of substantial literature interest in the context of breast surgery analgesia. PECS I targets the pectoral nerves in the fascial plane between pectoralis major and pectoralis minor. PECS II extends coverage to the serratus anterior plane, targeting medial and lateral pectoral nerves and the long thoracic nerve.

Several reviews and trials describe opioid-sparing effects associated with PECS blocks in breast reconstruction settings, although effect size, certainty, and generalizability vary across studies (Abdallah et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2018). ASRA-affiliated literature discusses these techniques within a broader framework of regional anesthesia expansion in thoracic and breast surgery. Dexamethasone as a perineural adjuvant is discussed in several sources as potentially extending block duration, though optimal dosing and route remain subjects of ongoing investigation.

### 2.2 Serratus Anterior Plane Block

The serratus anterior plane (SAP) block has been described as an alternative or complement to PECS II, with reported coverage of the lateral chest wall (Kim et al., 2018). Some authors describe it as accessible in cases where the PECS II plane is difficult to identify anatomically, though comparative evidence between techniques remains limited.

### 2.3 Reported Considerations and Limitations

Published literature discusses several considerations relevant to regional anesthesia in this setting.

Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) is a recognized risk with large-volume regional techniques. Published ASRA guidance on LAST prevention, recognition, and management serves as a widely used reference framework for this topic, and includes discussion of 20% lipid emulsion availability as part of institutional preparedness.

Bilateral procedures require attention to cumulative local anesthetic dosing. The literature discusses weight-based dosing principles and the importance of accounting for combined systemic and regional local anesthetic exposure when intravenous infusions are used concurrently.

Patient-specific factors including coagulopathy, anticoagulation status, local infection, and anatomic variation are described in the literature as relevant to regional anesthesia eligibility, with ASRA anticoagulation guidance serving as a primary reference framework for institutional decision-making.

## Section 3: Opioid-Sparing and Opioid-Free General Anesthesia

### 3.1 Conceptual Framing in the Literature

Opioid-free anesthesia (OFA) and opioid-sparing anesthesia (OSA) are terms used with some variability across publications to describe approaches that minimize or eliminate intraoperative opioid administration in favor of non-opioid analgesic combinations (Beloeil, 2020). The evidence base supporting these approaches is described as moderate to promising in some published reviews, while other authors note that benefit consistency and generalizability remain uncertain across patient populations and procedural settings.

The rationale discussed in published literature includes potential reduction in opioid-related adverse effects — particularly PONV, respiratory depression, and opioid-induced hyperalgesia — as well as alignment with opioid stewardship principles. Whether these benefits are realized in specific clinical contexts depends on patient selection, analgesic combination, and institutional factors.

### 3.2 Intravenous Analgesic Adjuncts

Several intravenous agents are discussed in the perioperative literature as components of opioid-sparing frameworks:

**Dexmedetomidine** is an alpha-2 agonist with sedative, analgesic, and opioid-sparing properties described across multiple perioperative publications (Weerink et al., 2017). The literature also discusses its hemodynamic effects — particularly bradycardia and hypotension — as important clinical considerations. The balance of potential analgesic benefit against hemodynamic risk is described as patient-specific and requiring attending-level clinical judgment.

**Intravenous lidocaine infusion** is discussed as having analgesic, anti-inflammatory, and opioid-sparing properties in several surgical contexts (McCarthy et al., 2010). Its applicability to breast surgery is discussed in the literature with some supporting evidence, though the evidence base is less robust than in abdominal surgery settings. Hepatic metabolism and cardiac conduction considerations are described as relevant factors in patient selection.

**Ketamine** at subanesthetic doses is discussed in published literature as an NMDA antagonist with analgesic and opioid-sparing properties (Devereaux et al., 2018). Several authors note that analgesic efficacy appears heterogeneous and may be influenced by individual patient factors. Contraindications discussed in the literature include active psychosis, uncontrolled hypertension, and certain cardiac conditions.

### 3.3 Propofol Total Intravenous Anesthesia

Propofol-based TIVA is discussed in published literature as associated with reduced PONV compared with volatile anesthetic maintenance, particularly in high-risk patients (Gan et al., 2020). This property is cited in ERAS-aligned perioperative literature as a rationale for TIVA consideration in settings where PONV prevention is a clinical priority, though the attending team determines appropriateness for individual patients.

## Section 4: Opioid-Inclusive Approaches — Reported Context

### 4.1 Continued Role in Selected Populations

Published literature does not characterize opioid-inclusive anesthesia as categorically suboptimal in all contexts. Several sources discuss populations in which opioid-containing approaches may represent an appropriate and clinically supported option — including patients on chronic opioid therapy, those with multiple contraindications to non-opioid adjuncts, and those in whom opioid-sparing strategies have previously proven inadequate.

The literature also discusses the ethical and clinical importance of adequate perioperative analgesia in patients with opioid tolerance, noting that inadequate pain management in these patients carries its own patient safety implications. Coordination with chronic prescribers and pain management specialists is discussed as important in these contexts.

### 4.2 Opioid Minimization Within Conventional Approaches

Even within opioid-containing frameworks, published ERAS literature commonly discusses opioid minimization principles, including reduced opioid exposure where feasible and combination with non-opioid multimodal adjuncts. This framing reflects the broader opioid stewardship orientation present throughout contemporary perioperative literature.

### 4.3 PONV in Opioid-Inclusive Contexts

Published literature commonly identifies postoperative opioid exposure as an important contributor to PONV risk (Gan et al., 2020). The Apfel simplified risk score, which incorporates postoperative opioid use as a risk factor among others, is the most widely referenced PONV risk stratification tool in perioperative guidelines. Multi-agent PONV prophylaxis is described in consensus publications as appropriate for patients at moderate to high risk.

## Section 5: Comparative Considerations Reported in the Literature

### 5.1 How Authors Frame the Comparison

Published literature discussing the comparison between opioid-sparing and opioid-inclusive approaches generally does not present a binary choice, but rather a spectrum of approaches calibrated to patient-specific factors. The framing in most ERAS-aligned publications is that regional and multimodal approaches are broadly discussed as potentially advantageous where feasible, with opioid-containing strategies retained as appropriate alternatives when opioid-sparing approaches are contraindicated, declined by patients, or technically not possible (PROSPECT Working Group, 2022).

### 5.2 Factors Discussed as Relevant in the Literature

The literature discusses several patient and procedural factors as potentially relevant to analgesic approach considerations in this setting:

- PONV risk profile and individual risk factors
- Coagulopathy or anticoagulation status as relevant to regional anesthesia feasibility
- Chronic opioid use and tolerance
- Comorbidities including obstructive sleep apnea, hepatic dysfunction, renal impairment, and cardiac conditions
- Procedure complexity and anticipated dissection extent
- Patient preferences regarding needle-based procedures
- Institutional formulary, local anesthetic availability, and regional anesthesia expertise

These factors are discussed in the literature as informing individualized clinical decision-making under attending supervision. They are presented here as educational context, not as a decision framework.

### 5.3 Recovery Outcomes Discussed in the Literature

Published studies examining opioid-sparing perioperative approaches in breast surgery contexts generally report outcomes including postoperative pain scores, PONV incidence, opioid consumption, time to discharge readiness, and patient satisfaction. Effect sizes and confidence in findings vary across studies depending on design quality, population characteristics, and outcome definitions. Readers are directed to primary sources for specific outcome data.

### 5.4 Important Uncertainties

Several important uncertainties are noted across the literature:

- Most studies are conducted at academic centers with specific regional anesthesia expertise; generalizability to community settings is uncertain
- Patient populations vary substantially across studies in terms of comorbidity burden, procedure complexity, and baseline opioid exposure
- Outcome definitions — particularly for PONV and "opioid-free" — are inconsistent across publications
- Long-term outcomes beyond the immediate perioperative period are less well-characterized
- Optimal combinations of multimodal agents and their relative contributions remain subjects of ongoing investigation

## Section 6: PONV — Literature Summary

PONV is discussed extensively in the perioperative breast surgery literature as a clinically important outcome affecting patient comfort, recovery quality, and resource utilization. The Apfel simplified risk score is among the most widely cited risk stratification tools in this context (Gan et al., 2020).

Published consensus guidelines describe a tiered prophylaxis approach in which the number and combination of preventive agents are scaled to patient risk level. Agents discussed include 5-HT3 antagonists, glucocorticoids, neurokinin-1 antagonists, and transdermal scopolamine, among others. The general principle across published rescue antiemetic discussions is to avoid repeating the same drug class in sequential rescue attempts.

The relationship between anesthetic technique and PONV is discussed in terms of both opioid minimization and propofol TIVA, both of which are associated with reduced PONV in published literature compared with opioid-containing volatile anesthesia in high-risk populations (Gan et al., 2020).

## Section 7: Special Population Considerations in the Literature

Published literature discusses several patient populations as warranting particular attention in perioperative planning for breast surgery:

**Elderly and frail patients** are discussed with reference to increased sensitivity to sedative and analgesic agents, higher delirium risk, reduced hepatic and renal clearance, and the importance of thoughtful dose consideration and postoperative monitoring. Processed EEG monitoring is discussed in some publications as potentially useful for anesthetic depth titration in this population.

**Patients with obstructive sleep apnea** are discussed as a population in whom opioid-related respiratory depression risk may be elevated, and in whom enhanced postoperative monitoring — including continuous pulse oximetry — is discussed as a relevant clinical consideration in published perioperative safety literature.

**Patients on chronic opioid therapy** are discussed in terms of tolerance, opioid-induced hyperalgesia, and the importance of perioperative analgesic planning that accounts for baseline opioid requirements. Coordination with prescribing clinicians is described as important in this context.

**Patients with opioid use disorder in recovery** are discussed with attention to the ethical dimensions of opioid exposure in the perioperative setting, the potential value of opioid-sparing approaches in minimizing relapse risk where feasible, and the importance of involving addiction medicine or recovery support resources.

These population-specific considerations are drawn from published literature and are presented here for educational purposes only.

## Section 8: Limitations of This Review

This review has several important limitations that readers should consider.

This is a narrative educational review, not a systematic review. Source identification was not exhaustive, and no formal evidence-grading framework was applied. The review reflects the author's educational synthesis of available literature and should not be treated as a comprehensive or authoritative evidence assessment.

The author is a premedical student without clinical licensure or authority to provide clinical guidance. Interpretations reflect educational engagement with published sources rather than professional clinical judgment.

Evidence quality across the sources reviewed is heterogeneous. Guideline recommendations, randomized trial findings, observational data, and expert opinion are discussed without consistent formal grading. Readers should consult primary sources and qualified clinicians for any clinical purpose.

Institutional variability is substantial. Perioperative practices, formulary availability, regional anesthesia expertise, and patient populations vary across institutions in ways that significantly affect the applicability of any published findings. Nothing in this review should be read as applicable to any specific institution, clinical context, or patient population.

This review reflects literature available through late 2025. The perioperative medicine literature evolves continuously, and subsequent publications may modify or supersede the discussions summarized here.

## Conclusion

The published perioperative literature on breast reconstruction revision surgery reflects an active and evolving discussion about analgesic strategy, PONV prevention, regional anesthesia integration, and recovery optimization. Several themes recur across sources: the potential advantages of opioid-sparing and multimodal approaches in appropriately selected patients, the importance of regional anesthesia expertise and patient-specific selection in realizing those advantages, the retained role of opioid-containing approaches in specific clinical contexts, and the centrality of individualized attending-level clinical judgment in all perioperative decision-making.

This educational review is intended to synthesize those themes for academic learning purposes. It does not resolve the clinical questions it discusses, advocate for specific approaches, or substitute for the expertise of the licensed clinicians who bear responsibility for perioperative care.
